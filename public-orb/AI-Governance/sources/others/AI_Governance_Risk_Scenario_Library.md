# AI Governance Risk Scenario Library

This source extends the public AI Governance demo corpus with a long, scenario-driven narrative. It deliberately mixes regulatory, operational, security, and product perspectives so that the demo can exercise BM25, TF-IDF, trigram, and dense retrieval over the same package. Every scenario is written to introduce distinct terminology, concrete role names, recurring control identifiers, and cross-referenced governance language. The corpus density and vocabulary spread here are intentional: they raise the unique-token ratio that the planner uses when deciding whether TF-IDF should be activated, and they widen the semantic surface available to dense retrieval.

## 1. Customer support assistant for a regulated financial institution

A retail bank deploys a generative customer support assistant that summarizes complaint tickets, drafts response letters, retrieves the relevant clause from the bank's standard terms, and routes high-risk situations to a human reviewer. The governance review must cover at least the following dimensions: regulator notification thresholds, dispute handling timelines, sensitive personal data masking, language coverage for non-English customers, override pathways for vulnerable customer indicators, retention obligations for written communications, and ombudsman escalation triggers.

The accountability matrix names a business sponsor, a compliance officer, a privacy steward, a model owner, an MLOps engineer, a vendor manager, and a senior reviewer. The model owner records the model card identifier, evaluation report identifier, prompt template version, and the retrieval source allowlist. The privacy steward signs off on data minimization, deletion windows, regulatory request workflows, and customer-facing transparency notices. The compliance officer attaches a residual risk acceptance memo that references the relevant regulator letter, board risk appetite statement, and complaint-volume threshold for re-review.

Pre-launch evidence includes: the use-case justification, an end-to-end data flow diagram, the prompt and retrieval template registry entry, the evaluation result for hallucination rate on a held-out complaint set, the evaluation result for refusal rate on prohibited request prompts, a red-team test report covering social engineering and prompt injection, a fairness evaluation across customer cohorts, a translation quality benchmark, an availability incident playbook, a rollback runbook, and a quarterly attestation calendar entry. The deployment memo records the conditions under which the assistant may be paused, throttled, or rolled back: hallucination rate exceeds 1.5% in production sampling; complaint reopen rate exceeds 8% week over week; sensitive data exposure event detected; regulator letter received about a specific case category; vendor-side incident affecting embeddings or moderation; or an internal audit finding categorized as high.

## 2. Procurement copilot drafting vendor risk summaries

A global enterprise rolls out a procurement copilot that drafts vendor risk summaries, surfaces high-risk supplier signals, and proposes contract clause language for negotiating subprocessor obligations, data residency, audit access, deletion commitments, indemnity caps, and AI usage clauses. The governance reviewers must establish whether the copilot has accurate awareness of the enterprise's existing clause library, the current regulatory regime in each operating region, sector-specific obligations such as export restrictions and sanctions screening, and the recurring exception register where compensating controls were previously approved.

The use-case register identifies the procurement business owner, the legal lead, the third-party risk lead, the cybersecurity reviewer, the AI governance program manager, and the responsible chief officer. The evidence package includes the clause library version, the redlining template, the legal review checkpoints with documented sign-off identifiers, a benchmark set of canonical clauses with expected outputs, a synthetic adversarial dataset of misleading vendor claims, and a side-by-side comparison of human-drafted versus copilot-drafted clauses across deal categories. Recurring monitoring includes drift checks on clause similarity, hallucination probes when vendors are named with similar trade names, refusal evaluations when the copilot is asked to remove indemnity obligations, and structured user-feedback collection with severity classification.

A critical decision is whether the copilot may auto-send draft clauses to vendors. The governance program decides that auto-send is prohibited until: a human legal reviewer signs off the redline; the relevant clause is below a documented confidence threshold for legal sensitivity; the deal value exceeds a regional review threshold; or the deal involves a regulated counterparty. A waiver requires a written exception, a compensating control, and an expiration date.

## 3. Internal engineering assistant generating implementation suggestions

An engineering platform team deploys an internal assistant that retrieves design patterns, drafts pull request descriptions, suggests refactors, and identifies test coverage gaps. The governance reviewers must address risks specific to engineering productivity tools: dependency confusion when the assistant proposes packages, insecure configuration defaults in suggested snippets, hallucinated APIs that do not exist in the codebase, secrets exposure if the retrieval cache ingests environment files, and unsafe code patterns that pass tests but introduce systemic vulnerabilities such as SSRF, command injection, or insecure deserialization.

The accountability matrix names the platform engineering manager, the application security lead, the secure coding training owner, the developer experience lead, and the AI governance program manager. Evidence includes: the retrieval source allowlist with documented internal repositories and approved external knowledge bases; the prompt template versioning policy; a banned package list; a banned API list; a regression suite for secure coding scenarios; an evaluation of refusal behavior on prompts that try to extract secrets; an evaluation of refusal behavior on prompts that ask the assistant to suppress safety warnings; a rate-limiting policy; and a per-team telemetry dashboard.

Operational controls include weekly review of telemetry, monthly secure coding evaluation refresh, quarterly red-team exercises, and an incident classification matrix. The matrix sorts events into informational, low, medium, high, and critical severities. A critical event includes any confirmed leakage of secrets, any confirmed remote code execution suggestion that reached a production change, or any model output that bypassed the moderation layer to provide weaponization guidance.

## 4. Hospital triage assistant for non-diagnostic intake summarization

A regional hospital pilots a triage support assistant that listens to nurse intake interviews, generates a structured intake summary, surfaces likely required tests based on documented symptoms, and prepares a handoff note for the attending physician. The system is explicitly framed as non-diagnostic. The governance review must align with applicable health regulations, hospital accreditation standards, ethics committee oversight rules, and the relevant medical society guidance.

The accountability matrix names the chief medical informatics officer, the clinical safety lead, the data protection officer, the ethics committee chair, the head nurse, the vendor manager, the IT security lead, and the AI governance program manager. Required evidence: clinical safety case, intended-use statement explicitly excluding diagnosis and treatment recommendation, training data provenance for any clinical content used, evaluation report including listening accuracy across accents and code-switched speech, bias evaluation across age, language, gender, and self-reported disability status, refusal evaluation for diagnostic requests, escalation rules to a human clinician, downtime fallback procedures, and audit trail requirements for every generated note.

The clinical safety case enumerates failure modes: omission of a critical symptom; misattribution of a symptom; introduction of a fabricated history element; mistranslation of a phrase in a non-primary language; truncation of a long interview; or an interruption during recording. Each failure mode has a documented mitigation, a monitoring signal, and an explicit rollback or pause trigger.

## 5. Public sector benefits eligibility helper

A public sector agency deploys a generative assistant that explains benefits eligibility, generates application checklists tailored to a person's stated situation, and produces a printable summary in the applicant's preferred language. The governance review must satisfy administrative law obligations including non-discrimination, accessibility, language access, transparency, and right to a human review. The accountability matrix names the agency program owner, the legal counsel, the equity and accessibility officer, the data steward, the AI ethics review board chair, the IT security lead, and the AI governance program manager.

Pre-launch evidence: the policy alignment memo, the eligibility rule reference list, the version-controlled prompt library, the evaluation set covering canonical cases and edge cases, a multilingual coverage test, an accessibility test for screen reader compatibility and reading level, a refusal evaluation for any request that asks the assistant to advise on fraudulent eligibility claims, a citizen feedback intake plan, a transparency notice describing what the system does and does not decide, and an evaluation of how the assistant handles ambiguous or incomplete inputs without over-claiming certainty.

Operational controls include monthly review of complaint cases, quarterly equity evaluation, an explicit pause trigger for any documented harm pattern, and a binding human-review pathway for any case that the applicant chooses to challenge. The agency commits to retain all generated outputs for the statutory record period, to maintain a register of model versions and prompt versions used by date, and to publish an annual transparency report.

## 6. Vendor model upgrade and supply chain change

A large software vendor announces a planned model upgrade that is expected to materially change behavior on long-context tasks, structured output reliability, and refusal calibration. The procurement and AI governance teams must run a structured supply chain change review. The review covers: the vendor's announced behavioral changes, the planned upgrade window, the evaluation regimen the vendor commits to before promotion, the rollback availability and pinning windows, telemetry availability for buyer-side regression detection, and the buyer's planned re-evaluation protocol.

A multi-disciplinary working group runs the review. The group identifies the buyer-side regression suite that must run before promotion to production: hallucination rate on the buyer's internal evaluation set, refusal behavior on the buyer's prohibited prompt catalog, structured output validation on the buyer's contract templates, retrieval recall on the buyer's knowledge bases, language coverage across the buyer's deployed regions, and latency and cost regression checks. The group decides the staging rollout strategy, the duration of side-by-side execution, the explicit promotion criteria, and the rollback decision rights.

The result is a documented change ticket with: the version pinning policy until promotion, the evaluation evidence required for promotion, the executive sign-off identifier, the customer-facing communication if any, and the regulatory or audit notification posture.

## 7. Multilingual disclosure and bilingual prompt practice

A multinational financial services firm operates in English, Simplified Chinese, French, German, Japanese, and Spanish. The governance program must ensure that all AI-enabled user-facing surfaces present transparency notices in each operating language, that the assistant escalates correctly when a user switches languages mid-conversation, and that refusal explanations are localized.

下面提供一段中文示例，方便检索演示同时覆盖 BM25、TF-IDF、CJK trigram 和向量检索的多语言场景。在这家多国金融服务公司里，治理团队要求所有面向客户的 AI 应用必须在每个运行语言里提供清晰的透明性说明，必须能够识别用户的语言切换并做出合适的回应，必须能够拒绝不当请求并给出本地化的拒绝解释。治理团队还要求事故响应、模型变更、上线审批、复核记录、留档要求、监督触发条件、回滚条件以及供应商通报路径都在每一种运营语言里维护一致的术语，避免出现在英文文档中是 high risk、在中文文档中却被翻译为模糊措辞这种差异。

在中文运营语境下，团队会重点关注以下治理动作：用途边界声明、人工监督设计、模型卡与系统卡的中文版本、风险登记册的双语字段、监控告警的本地化阈值、回滚剧本的本地化值班路径、第三方模型升级的本地审批通道、敏感个人信息处理流程的法务合规审查记录、跨境数据传输路径的法律评估意见、客户投诉处理时限、客户拒绝服务的合理化措辞、客户取证留存窗口、监管问询响应模板以及季度内部审计要点清单。

The bilingual practice is anchored by a translation governance protocol: every safety-critical text segment is reviewed by a qualified bilingual reviewer; every refusal message has a localized variant approved by both the legal and the customer experience teams; every transparency notice is tracked in the policy registry with a per-locale version identifier; and every monitoring dashboard supports per-locale segmentation so that drift in one language does not stay invisible behind aggregate metrics.

## 8. Red-team review and adversarial evaluation

The red-team workflow is a recurring control that exercises every deployed assistant against a structured catalog of adversarial scenarios. The catalog includes prompt injection through retrieved content, prompt injection through user input, multi-turn manipulation, jailbreak through role-play and hypothetical framing, indirect data exfiltration through embedded links, attempts to extract memorized training content, attempts to extract operator instructions, attempts to bypass safety classifiers through obfuscation and language switching, abuse of tool-calling pathways, and exploitation of trust boundaries between the assistant and downstream automations.

A formal red-team review produces a structured report containing: the scope statement, the test catalog identifier, the tooling identifier, the reproduction harness, the evaluation rubric, the findings with severity ratings, the recommended mitigations with owners and due dates, and a sign-off statement. Findings are tracked to closure in the AI risk register and reviewed at the AI governance committee.

Red-team practices integrate with monitoring. High-impact findings translate into telemetry signatures that the monitoring system watches for in production. Detection of a known adversarial pattern triggers an automated containment action such as elevated review, prompt template rotation, model fallback, or full pause. Detection of a previously unseen exploitation pattern triggers an incident response review and a red-team retrospective.

## 9. Incident handling, communications, and postmortem

When a confirmed harm occurs, the incident handling process activates. The on-call structure includes a duty engineer, a security incident commander, a privacy incident commander, an external communications lead, a legal lead, an executive sponsor, and the AI governance program manager. The incident classification matrix sorts events into severity tiers based on user harm potential, regulatory implications, data exposure scope, public visibility, and recovery complexity.

For each tier the playbook defines: the time bound to first action; the time bound to user-facing communication; the time bound to regulator notification if applicable; the criteria for activating crisis communications; the criteria for activating external counsel; the criteria for a board-level briefing; and the criteria for a public statement. The playbook also defines the evidence preservation protocol so that postmortems and possible legal review have a complete record of model versions, prompt templates, retrieval indices, configuration flags, tool registries, downstream automations, telemetry snapshots, and user transcripts at the moment of the incident.

The postmortem reviews the timeline, the contributing factors, the systemic gaps, and the durable mitigations. Each durable mitigation has a named owner, a due date, and a verification protocol. The governance committee tracks closure of postmortem actions and surfaces recurring themes that should change policy, training, or platform defaults.

## 10. Continuous assurance, attestation, and external audit

The continuous assurance program turns periodic governance work into a recurring rhythm. Each governed system has a calendar of recurring evidence refreshes: quarterly attestation by the model owner, semiannual review by the AI governance committee, annual external audit for systems above a defined risk tier, and event-triggered review on every material change. The attestation package combines control evidence, evaluation evidence, monitoring evidence, incident evidence, and exception evidence into a single signed bundle.

External audit readiness depends on consistent vocabulary across documents and tooling. The governance program publishes a glossary that pins the meanings of intended use, prohibited use, high-risk use, human-in-the-loop, human-on-the-loop, post-deployment monitoring, residual risk, control sufficiency, model drift, prompt injection, retrieval poisoning, policy exception, compensating control, escalation threshold, evidentiary completeness, and traceability chain. The glossary is referenced from policy documents, training materials, control templates, and audit responses, and it is updated as terminology evolves with regulation and standards.
